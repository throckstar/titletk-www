STORY STRATEGY

Themes and
Storylines.

CodeSignal has a credible POV, proprietary data and insights, and a moment in the market that makes it timely.

Story 01 · Tigran's POV

AI is reshaping the talent pipeline, and skills development is the response.

As AI automates entry-level tasks, companies are reducing junior hiring. The downstream effect is a thinning senior pipeline, since most experienced practitioners developed foundational skills earlier in their careers. Entry-level expectations have jumped to competency levels that previously took five to seven years to develop, and the skills to meet those expectations don't yet exist at scale. Tigran has been building this argument publicly through Deep View, the University of Michigan, and a consistent LinkedIn presence. The next step is sharpening it into a pitchable narrative with a clear angle and two or three supporting data points, so it travels beyond the audiences already following him.

Story 02 · Owned Data

CodeSignal's annual ranking identifies engineering talent that traditional rankings consistently overlook.

14 of the top 50 schools in the 2026 report don't appear in the US News ranking, a finding with real implications for how hiring teams and universities think about talent pipelines. Forbes and NBC Bay Area covered the report well. Amy Shackelford and Mary Papazian give it genuine depth beyond a single spokesperson. Building reporter relationships earlier in the cycle (ahead of next year's release rather than at launch) is the natural next step for a story this strong.

Story 03 · AI Interviewing

CodeSignal's agentic assessment data defines what AI-era hiring looks like in practice.

91% of engineers use agentic AI tools. 75% shipped production code with AI assistance in the past six months. 56% of hiring managers are now hesitant to hire engineers who don't. Organizations using Claude Code and Cursor in their assessments are evaluating candidates under real working conditions, and CodeSignal has the longitudinal data to contextualize what that means for hiring standards. The team has handled recent inbound from The Verge, Business Insider, and Bloomberg well. Building a proactive pitch strategy alongside that inbound is the logical extension of what is already working.

Additional themes.

These connect to FY27 themes and the product roadmap. Each strengthens as data and product capabilities develop over the next six to twelve months.

Cheating attempts in assessments doubled in 2025, with entry-level roles hit hardest.
Industry-wide, cheating attempts moved from 16% to 35%. Entry-level went from 15% to 40%. FastCo covered the 2025 findings. Building reporter relationships ahead of the next release (rather than distributing after) will extend its reach considerably.
CodeSignal has been building toward skills-based hiring since before it became a consensus view.
McKinsey and Deloitte are writing about it now. Federal workforce policy is moving in that direction. CodeSignal's decade of actual outcome data (not survey research) gives it a credibility position that is difficult for newer entrants to match. That is a meaningful frame for a Series D raise.
Self-serve Hire, launched March 2026, makes skills-based assessment accessible beyond the enterprise.
Assessment science that previously required enterprise procurement is now available to smaller organizations. Adam Vassar and Brian Genisio are the natural voices for this lane. Cosmo connects the individual learning track to the same story.

Marquee Activation · 01

The Signal.

Like the Bat-Signal, but for the future of work.

Ahead of CodeSignal's next major product launch, the CodeSignal mark gets projected across San Francisco: on the Bay Bridge, the Salesforce Tower, buildings in SoMa. No context. No hints. Just the symbol.

AI Twitter notices first. Then the press. Reporters already under embargo know exactly what is coming. Everyone else starts asking.

Using the upcoming Skills Transfer Index as an example: on launch day, the signal gets its answer. A new way to see what skills are worth and where they can take you. The stunt becomes the visual shorthand for the launch, timed to other events in San Francisco for additional exposure and brand lift.

Content Activation · 02

"The Skills That
Got Me Here."

Partner with campus creators (Voodies and similar) to visit CodeSignal's dark-horse universities — Stony Brook, San Jose State, Santa Clara — and film a two-part question series that celebrates preparedness rather than exposes gaps.

Part 1: Soft questions that establish the university's identity. Students relax. The school gets positive airtime. Part 2: Real workplace scenario questions. The students answer well because CodeSignal prepared them. You film their competent responses. You capture the moment they realize they actually know this.

Each video ends with a reveal: "This university ranks #2 in the country for job-ready skills, even though you've probably never heard of it." Flash the CodeSignal ranking data. Link to the full report.

Release one university per week. Each school's marketing team amplifies their own episode. Build bracket-style tournament energy around which underrated program has the most job-ready students. End every video with "See how your skills stack up" linking to a free CodeSignal assessment.
